cond Expressions

  • Question 1

    • Build a cond expression equivalent to the above nested if expressions 

      (define (mag x)
  (cond [(> x 0) "positive"]
        [(= x 0) "zero"]
        [else "negative"]))
  • Question 2

    • We have seen that cond is used instead of if when we have multiple parallel cases. For example, as seen in Question 1: 

      (define (mag1 x)
  (if (< x 0)
      "negative"
      (if (= x 0)
          "zero"
          "positive")))

    • The above can be re-written as: 

      (define (mag2 x)
  (cond [(< x 0) "negative"]
        [(= x 0) "zero"]
        [else    "positive"]))
    • Now let's hand step the call to (mag2 0)

      • What is the next step of the evaluation of: 

        (cond [(< 0 0) "negative"]
      [(= 0 0) "zero"]
      [else "positive"])

        1.  (cond [true    "negative"]
       [(= 0 0) "zero"]
       [else    "positive"])

        2.  (cond [false   "negative"]
       [(= 0 0) "zero"]
       [else    "positive"])

        3. (cond [(= 0 0) "zero"]
      [else    "positive"])
      • The answer is #2.
  • Question 3
    • After the previous step of the evaluation we are left with:
      • What is the next step of the evaluation? 
        1.  (cond [(= 0 0) "zero"]
       [else    "positive"])

        2.  (cond [false "negative"]
       [true  "zero"]
       [else  "positive"])

        3. "zero"
      • The answer is #1.
  • Question 4
    • After the previous step of the evaluation we are left with:
      • What is the next step of the evaluation. 
        1.  (cond [else "positive"])

        2.  (cond [true "zero"]
       [else "positive"])

        3. "zero"
      • The answer is #2.
  • Question 5
    • After the previous step of the evaluation we are left with:
      • What is the next step of the evaluation? 
        1.  (cond [true "zero"])

        2.  (cond [else "positive"])

        3. "zero"
      • The answer is #3.
